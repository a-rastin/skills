# Structures for Persian Academic Documents

Choose the structure required by the target institution or venue. These outlines are defaults, not substitutes for official templates.

## Thesis or dissertation

### 1. Governing structure

A thesis or dissertation should be organized in the official institutional sequence, with a clear logical thread from the research problem to the objectives, methodology, findings, discussion, and conclusion. The literature review must synthesize evidence and establish the study gap rather than present disconnected summaries of individual sources.

### 2. Required order of thesis components

Arrange the thesis in the following order:

1. Persian front cover.
2. Blank page.
3. “In the Name of God” page.
4. Persian title page containing the information printed on the cover.
5. Student declaration.
6. Copyright, reproduction rights, and ownership-of-results statement.
7. Dedication and acknowledgments, normally limited to one page.
8. Persian abstract and keywords.
9. List of symbols and abbreviations, when needed.
10. Table of contents.
11. List of tables, when needed.
12. List of figures, charts, photographs, and maps, when needed.
13. Chapter One: introduction, general background, and problem statement.
14. Chapter Two: literature review.
15. Chapter Three: materials and methods.
16. Chapter Four: findings/results.
17. Chapter Five: discussion and conclusion.
18. References.
19. Appendices, such as supplementary tables, figures, questionnaires, forms, or instruments, when needed.
20. Supervisor’s confirmation that the student is ready to defend.
21. Thesis-defense minutes or approval form.
22. English declaration.
23. English abstract and keywords.
24. English title page.
25. Final blank page.
26. English back cover.

### 3. Persian abstract and keywords

The Persian abstract should not exceed 600 words. It should be concise but sufficiently informative and normally contain:

- background or introduction and the study purpose;
- materials and methods;
- principal findings; and
- conclusion.

Provide three to five keywords. Separate Persian keywords with the Persian semicolon «؛» and, where possible, select terms that correspond to Medical Subject Headings (MeSH). The English abstract should accurately correspond to the Persian abstract and should not introduce results or claims absent from the Persian version.

### 4. Main chapters

#### Chapter One: Introduction, general background, and problem statement

Establish the context of the research and explain why the study is necessary. The chapter should develop a coherent progression through the following elements, as applicable:

1. background and scientific or clinical context;
2. precise statement of the problem;
3. summary and synthesis of the existing evidence;
4. unresolved issue, ambiguity, or evidence gap addressed by the study;
5. significance and necessity of the research;
6. study aim and specific objectives;
7. research questions or hypotheses, where appropriate;
8. scope and delimitations; and
9. conceptual and operational definitions of key terms or variables, when needed.

The problem statement must make the contribution or novelty of the study explicit. It should not merely state that a topic is important.

#### Chapter Two: Literature review and theoretical/conceptual foundations

Include both:

1. the theoretical or conceptual foundations of the study; and
2. a critical review of relevant scientific studies.

Organize the review by concepts, themes, debates, methods, populations, outcomes, or chronology, depending on the research question. Compare studies across their designs, samples, measures, findings, and limitations. Use synthesis paragraphs supported by multiple related sources rather than one paragraph per article. Conclude with the specific gap that logically motivates the current study.

Together, Chapters One and Two should not normally exceed 30% of the total thesis text.

#### Chapter Three: Materials and methods

Describe the methodology in sufficient detail to permit scientific evaluation and, where applicable, replication. Include the following elements as relevant to the study design:

1. study design and methodological approach;
2. setting, location, and study period;
3. target population, study population, or research materials;
4. inclusion and exclusion criteria;
5. sampling method and sample-size determination;
6. variables, outcomes, exposures, predictors, and operational definitions;
7. data-collection instruments and measurement procedures;
8. validity and reliability of instruments, or trustworthiness criteria for qualitative research;
9. data-collection process and study procedures;
10. quality-control procedures and management of bias;
11. statistical or qualitative analysis methods, including software and decision thresholds when relevant; and
12. ethical approval, informed consent, confidentiality, and other ethical safeguards.

Every objective or research question should have a corresponding method and analysis plan.

#### Chapter Four: Findings/results

Present the findings in a classified, logical order that follows the objectives, research questions, hypotheses, or prespecified analytical sequence. Clearly distinguish descriptive findings from inferential findings.

- Report participant or sample flow where relevant.
- Present baseline or descriptive characteristics before outcome analyses.
- Use tables and figures only when they improve comprehension.
- Report estimates with appropriate measures of uncertainty and exact statistical results where applicable.
- Avoid detailed interpretation, speculation, causal explanation, or extensive comparison with prior literature in this chapter.
- Do not repeat the same numerical information unnecessarily in the text, tables, and figures.

#### Chapter Five: Discussion and conclusion

Interpret the findings in relation to the study objectives and current scientific evidence. The chapter should normally include:

1. concise summary of the principal findings, organized by objective or research question;
2. interpretation of each major finding;
3. comparison with relevant previous studies;
4. explanation of agreements and differences, with consideration of design, population, measurement, context, bias, and chance;
5. scientific, clinical, educational, policy, or practical implications, as appropriate;
6. calibrated discussion of causality and generalizability;
7. study strengths;
8. study limitations and their likely effects on interpretation;
9. final conclusion that directly answers the research question; and
10. evidence-based recommendations for practice and future research.

Do not introduce new results in the conclusion. Recommendations must arise from the findings, limitations, or defensible practical implications of the study.

### 5. References and appendices

For a Persian-language thesis, format references according to the Vancouver style unless the university, faculty, or program supplies a more specific rule. For an English-language thesis, use Harvard style when required by the institutional guideline.

Appendices may contain supplementary tables, figures, charts, questionnaires, checklists, consent forms, data-collection tools, approvals, or other supporting documents that are necessary but would interrupt the flow of the main text.

### 6. Page layout and typography

Apply the following TUMS formatting rules unless a faculty-specific template overrides them:

- Paper: white A4 paper, 21 × 29.7 cm, approximately 100 g/m².
- Printing: the thesis text should be printed double-sided; official forms should be printed single-sided.
- Chapter opening: each chapter or major section should begin on an odd-numbered page.
- Odd pages: right margin 3.5 cm, left margin 2.5 cm, top margin 2.5 cm, bottom margin 2.5 cm.
- Even pages: right margin 2.5 cm, left margin 3.5 cm, top margin 2.5 cm, bottom margin 2.5 cm.
- Title pages: right margin 3.5 cm, left margin 2.5 cm, top margin 10 cm, bottom margin 10 cm.
- Persian body text: B Nazanin, 14 pt.
- English body text: Times New Roman, 12 pt.
- Main Persian headings: B Titr, 16 pt.
- Main English headings: Times New Roman, 14 pt, bold.
- Line spacing: 1.15.
- First-line paragraph indentation: 1 cm.
- Footnotes: same typeface as the body text, 10 pt.

Use equivalent licensed or institution-approved fonts only when the prescribed fonts are unavailable, and verify that Persian shaping, numerals, punctuation, and right-to-left behavior remain correct.

### 7. Pagination, tables, figures, and formulas

- Use Abjad letters for the Persian preliminary pages through the lists, where required by the institutional template.
- Use Arabic/Persian numerals for the main thesis text.
- Place page numbers at the bottom center of the page.
- Number every table, figure, chart, photograph, and map consistently by chapter or according to the official template.
- Place table numbers and titles above tables.
- Place figure, chart, photograph, and map numbers and captions below the corresponding item.
- Keep formulas within the permitted text area and align them according to disciplinary convention while preserving the right-to-left integrity of the surrounding Persian text.

### 8. Punctuation and language consistency

- Attach periods, commas, semicolons, and colons to the preceding word and insert one space after them.
- Do not place spaces immediately inside parentheses.
- Mark direct quotations clearly and cite their sources.
- Use consistent Persian orthography, half-spaces, terminology, numerals, abbreviations, and transliteration throughout the thesis.
- Define abbreviations at first use and include a list of abbreviations when their number justifies it.

### 9. Thesis alignment audit

Before submission, verify the following chain:

- Problem → documented evidence gap → study purpose.
- Purpose → specific objective, research question, or hypothesis.
- Objective → corresponding variable, procedure, and analysis.
- Method → reported result.
- Result → discussion and interpretation.
- Conclusion → supported result.
- Recommendation → finding, limitation, or defensible implication.

Also verify that:

- every objective is answered in the results and conclusion;
- every reported analysis was described in the methods;
- the discussion does not overstate observational or uncertain evidence;
- tables and figures are cited in the text and are not redundant;
- the Persian and English titles, abstracts, keywords, and study details are mutually consistent; and
- all required institutional forms and end matter are present in the prescribed order.

## Journal or conference paper

Use the venue's author guidelines. A common empirical structure is:

- **Title:** specific, searchable, and proportionate to the evidence.
- **Abstract:** problem/context, purpose, essential method, principal results, and conclusion; use a structured abstract only when required.
- **Keywords:** complement rather than merely repeat the title.
- **Introduction:** establish context, synthesize the gap, explain significance, and state the objective/question/hypothesis.
- **Methods:** enable evaluation and, when applicable, replication; report ethics and analysis decisions.
- **Results:** report findings in the order promised by aims and methods; avoid unsupported interpretation.
- **Discussion:** interpret rather than repeat results; compare evidence, consider alternatives, state limitations, and define implications.
- **Conclusion:** give the defensible answer and contribution without new evidence.

Include required funding, conflict-of-interest, ethics, author-contribution, data-availability, and acknowledgment statements when applicable.

## Research proposal

Match the funder or university form. Common sections include:

1. Title and concise project summary.
2. Background and problem statement.
3. Evidence-based gap and rationale.
4. Aim, specific objectives, research questions, and hypotheses where appropriate.
5. Conceptual or theoretical framework.
6. Methods aligned with objectives: design, data/materials, sampling, measures, procedures, analysis, ethics, and risk mitigation.
7. Innovation and expected scholarly or practical contribution.
8. Feasibility: team, resources, timeline, milestones, and budget justification.
9. Limitations, contingency plans, dissemination, and data management where required.
10. References and appendices.

Write proposals prospectively. Distinguish expected outcomes from existing results, and never present aspirations as established effects.

## Literature review

- State the scope, question, and organizing principle.
- Describe search and selection methods when claiming systematic or scoping status.
- Compare themes, theories, methods, populations, findings, and limitations across sources.
- Use synthesis paragraphs with multiple related sources rather than one paragraph per paper.
- Conclude with a specific gap that logically motivates the study.

## Alignment audit

- Problem → gap → purpose.
- Purpose → research question/objective.
- Objective → method and analysis.
- Result → corresponding objective.
- Conclusion → supported result.
- Recommendation → evidence, limitation, or practical implication.
