# Structures for Persian Academic Documents

Choose the structure required by the target institution or venue. These outlines are defaults, not substitutes for official templates.

## Thesis or dissertation

1. Preliminary matter: title pages, declarations, acknowledgments, Persian abstract, keywords, contents, and lists of tables/figures.
2. Introduction and problem statement: context, research gap, problem, importance, aims, questions or hypotheses, scope, and definitions.
3. Literature review and theoretical/conceptual framework: organize by concepts, debates, methods, or chronology; synthesize rather than catalogue sources.
4. Methodology: design, setting, population/sample or materials, variables/measures, procedures, analysis, validity/reliability or trustworthiness, and ethics.
5. Results/findings: follow research questions or analytical logic; separate observation from interpretation where the field expects it.
6. Discussion: interpret findings, compare prior work, explain agreements and differences, state implications, and calibrate claims.
7. Conclusion and recommendations: answer the question, summarize contribution, acknowledge limitations, and propose defensible future work.
8. References and appendices.

Maintain a consistent thread from problem to question, method, result, and conclusion. Do not let the literature review become unrelated source summaries.

## Journal or conference paper

Use the venue's author guidelines. A common empirical structure is:

- **Title:** specific, searchable, and proportionate to the evidence.
- **Abstract:** problem/context, purpose, essential method, principal results, and conclusion; use a structured abstract only when required.
- **Keywords:** complement rather than merely repeat the title.
- **Introduction:** establish context, synthesize the gap, explain significance, and state the objective/question/hypothesis.
- **Methods:** enable evaluation and, when applicable, replication; report ethics and analysis decisions.
- **Results:** report findings in the order promised by aims and methods; avoid unsupported interpretation.
- **Discussion:** interpret rather than repeat results; compare evidence, consider alternatives, state limitations, and define implications.
- **Conclusion:** give the defensible answer and contribution without new evidence.

Include required funding, conflict-of-interest, ethics, author-contribution, data-availability, and acknowledgment statements when applicable.

## Research proposal

Match the funder or university form. Common sections include:

1. Title and concise project summary.
2. Background and problem statement.
3. Evidence-based gap and rationale.
4. Aim, specific objectives, research questions, and hypotheses where appropriate.
5. Conceptual or theoretical framework.
6. Methods aligned with objectives: design, data/materials, sampling, measures, procedures, analysis, ethics, and risk mitigation.
7. Innovation and expected scholarly or practical contribution.
8. Feasibility: team, resources, timeline, milestones, and budget justification.
9. Limitations, contingency plans, dissemination, and data management where required.
10. References and appendices.

Write proposals prospectively. Distinguish expected outcomes from existing results, and never present aspirations as established effects.

## Literature review

- State the scope, question, and organizing principle.
- Describe search and selection methods when claiming systematic or scoping status.
- Compare themes, theories, methods, populations, findings, and limitations across sources.
- Use synthesis paragraphs with multiple related sources rather than one paragraph per paper.
- Conclude with a specific gap that logically motivates the study.

## Alignment audit

- Problem → gap → purpose.
- Purpose → research question/objective.
- Objective → method and analysis.
- Result → corresponding objective.
- Conclusion → supported result.
- Recommendation → evidence, limitation, or practical implication.
